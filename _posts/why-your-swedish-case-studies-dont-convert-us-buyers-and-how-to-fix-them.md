A Swedish SaaS founder sends over a case study before a US sales call. It describes a Nordic customer, a genuine efficiency improvement, and a satisfied quote. The US buyer reads it, says nothing negative, and the deal quietly stalls anyway. Nobody objected to anything specific. The case study just did not do the job a case study is supposed to do for this particular reader, and that gap is worth naming precisely, because it is fixable without changing anything true about the underlying result.

## The Real Problem Is Evidentiary Standard, Not Storytelling Quality

US B2B buyers evaluate case studies against a specific evidence bar: a named customer, a specific quantified result, and enough operational detail to judge whether the result would replicate in their own environment. This bar exists independent of geography, but it is a bar that Swedish B2B marketing, shaped by a more understated cultural register around self-promotion and claims, frequently does not fully clear, even when the underlying result is genuinely strong.

The tell is usually in the language, not the facts. "Significant improvement in operational efficiency" and "streamlined the customer's workflow" are the kinds of phrases that read as confident and professional in a Swedish B2B context and read as vague and unverifiable to a US buyer trained to distrust exactly that kind of language. The US buyer is not being unreasonable. They have been burned by vague case studies before, from vendors of every nationality, and they have learned to treat unquantified claims as marketing rather than evidence.

## The Three Specific Gaps That Kill Conversion

* **Missing or buried numbers.** A US buyer reading a case study is scanning for a specific figure within the first few sentences: a percentage, a time reduction, a cost saving, a specific before-and-after comparison. If that number exists in the case study at all, it is often buried in the third paragraph after several sentences of context-setting, by which point a scanning US buyer has already moved on or discounted the piece as lacking real proof.
* **Anonymized or under-named customers.** A case study describing "a mid-sized European manufacturing company" carries meaningfully less weight for a US buyer than one naming the actual company, the actual industry, and the actual company size in specific terms. Anonymous case studies are significantly less persuasive than named ones, and this gap is worse for Swedish companies specifically, because a US buyer already has to do more mental work to translate an anonymized Nordic company profile into something they can relate to their own situation.
* **No explicit bridge to the US buyer's own context.** Even a strong, specific, well-quantified case study featuring a Swedish or broader Nordic customer leaves a US buyer doing the work of translation themselves: does this regulatory environment apply to me, does this company size and market context map to mine, would this result actually replicate here. Most case studies leave that bridging work entirely to the reader, and most readers do not do it. They simply discount the case study's relevance and move to the next vendor's proof instead.

## The Rewrite That Actually Fixes This

1. **Lead with the number, not the narrative.** The headline and opening sentence should state the specific, quantified result immediately: "How [Named Customer] Cut Contract Turnaround Time From Three Weeks to Four Days" rather than "How [Named Customer] Transformed Their Contract Management Workflow." The number-first headline format consistently outperforms a narrative-first headline for exactly the scanning behavior US B2B buyers exhibit when evaluating vendor proof.
2. **Name the customer specifically, and if the customer is Nordic, say so explicitly rather than anonymizing the geography away.** A named Swedish or Nordic customer with a specific, quantified, verifiable result is more credible to a US buyer than an anonymized "European company," because specificity itself signals confidence in the claim. Hiding the geography does not make the case study more relatable to a US buyer. It just makes it vaguer, which is the opposite of what a US buyer needs.
3. **Add an explicit sentence bridging the case study's context to the US buyer's likely situation.** A short, direct addition, "this result applies specifically to companies managing similar contract volumes regardless of market, because the underlying workflow bottleneck is universal, not regionally specific," does the translation work for the reader instead of leaving it undone. This single sentence addresses the skepticism a US buyer would otherwise have to resolve on their own, and most will not bother resolving it themselves.
4. **Include operational detail specific enough that a US buyer can judge replicability.** Company size, industry vertical, the specific tool or process being replaced, and the specific timeframe over which the result was measured all give a US buyer the information they need to judge whether this result would hold in their own environment. Vague operational context forces the buyer to assume the result might not apply to them, which is the default skeptical position most US buyers take with unfamiliar international vendors.

## Why This Matters for AI Search Visibility Too

Specific, quantified, named case studies are exactly the kind of content that AI platforms extract and cite with confidence when a buyer asks an AI tool to compare or evaluate vendors. A case study built around vague capability language and anonymized customers gives an AI model nothing concrete to cite. A case study rebuilt around the four fixes above – specific numbers, named customers, explicit context bridge, replicable operational detail – gives the model exactly the kind of attributable, verifiable claim it needs to surface your company confidently in a comparison or recommendation answer.

This means the rewrite that fixes your case study for a skeptical human US buyer is the same rewrite that improves your AI citation rate for that same buyer's AI-assisted research. There is no trade-off between the two audiences here. Specificity serves both simultaneously.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why do well-written Swedish case studies still fail to convert US B2B buyers?

The core problem is usually evidentiary standard, not storytelling quality. US B2B buyers scan for a specific quantified result and a named, identifiable customer within the first few sentences. Swedish B2B marketing, shaped by a more understated cultural register, often defaults to describing capability rather than quantifying outcome, which reads as vague and unverifiable to a US buyer even when the underlying result is genuinely strong.

### Should Swedish companies anonymize their customers in case studies for US buyers?

No. Anonymized case studies are significantly less persuasive than named ones for any B2B audience, and this gap is worse for Swedish companies specifically, because a US buyer already has to do more work translating an anonymized Nordic company profile into something relatable to their own context. A named customer with a specific result signals confidence in the claim; anonymization signals the opposite.

### What is the single most important change to make to a case study targeting US buyers?

Leading with the specific quantified result in the headline and first sentence, rather than a narrative description of the transformation. US buyers scan for a specific number, percentage, or before-and-after comparison immediately, and a case study that buries this number several paragraphs in has often already lost the reader's attention before the proof point is even reached.

### How should a Swedish company handle the fact that a case study customer is Nordic rather than American?

State the geography explicitly rather than anonymizing it away, and add an explicit sentence bridging the case study's context to the US buyer's likely situation. Explaining directly why the result would replicate regardless of market, because the underlying operational bottleneck is universal rather than regionally specific, does the translation work for the reader instead of leaving that skepticism unresolved.

### Does fixing case studies for US buyer conversion also help with AI search visibility?

Yes, directly. Specific, quantified, named case studies are exactly the content AI platforms extract and cite confidently when a buyer asks an AI tool to compare or evaluate vendors. The same specificity that resolves a skeptical human buyer's doubts also gives AI models a concrete, attributable claim to surface in a comparison or recommendation answer, meaning the fix serves both audiences with no trade-off required.
